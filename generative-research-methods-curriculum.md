# Generative Research Methods

## A Curriculum for Framework Engineering

**Myth register:** The Breeder's Path
**Framework register:** Heterodox Structure Framework Engineering
**Formal register:** Generative Research Methods (GRM)

*Drafted July 3, 2026. Sammuel Pendry, with formalization by Claude (Anthropic). CC BY 4.0. This document is the curriculum published before the credential, on the principle that knowledge ships first and apparatus follows if the world wants it.*

## What This Trains

Most research training teaches people to be right. This curriculum teaches people to generate abundantly, store contradictions without panic, pre-register their own executions, let external reality do the selecting, and synthesize working structures from the survivors. The graduate is not a specialist in any domain. The graduate is someone who can enter any domain, breed a population of candidate frameworks, and run an honest selection process that leaves behind either a validated structure or a documented graveyard, both of which have value.

The method was not designed on a whiteboard. It was extracted from practice: a corpus of frameworks built across physics, algorithms, AI architecture, business, and philosophy, whose internal contradictions repeatedly turned out to contain the answers its tests later demanded. The curriculum reverse-engineers that practice into something transmissible.

One warning before the architecture. This program produces people who kill their own ideas in public. Students who need their ideas to survive in order to feel worthwhile will suffer here, and should either expect to be changed or choose another course of study.

## The Name Question

The program carries three names by design, one per register, and the register determines where each name may travel.

The myth name, **The Breeder's Path**, tells the story internally and never leaves the house. The framework name, **Heterodox Structure Framework Engineering**, describes the practice with full accuracy and carries one structural flaw as an external label: heterodoxy cannot survive its own accreditation, since a heterodoxy with a curriculum committee is an orthodoxy by construction. The word self-cancels at the moment the credential becomes real, so it stays in the middle register as the practice's honest description among practitioners.

Candidate formal names, each with what it buys and what it costs:

1. **Generative Research Methods.** Buys: sits legibly beside "quantitative methods" and "qualitative methods" in any catalog; describes the generation-selection loop without ideology. Costs: mild collision with generative AI branding, which is also a timing asset. Front-runner.
2. **Framework Engineering.** Buys: the shortest honest name; "engineer" signals build-and-test. Costs: software-adjacent readers will assume web frameworks for the first decade.
3. **Structural Synthesis.** Buys: precise about the compositional step. Costs: reads as chemistry or civil engineering to outsiders.
4. **Applied Epistemics.** Buys: philosophically exact. Costs: "epistemics" empties rooms outside philosophy departments.
5. **AI-Augmented Research Methods.** Buys: names the division of labor the 2026 market is actively creating demand for; the easiest sell to a university partner this decade. Costs: dates itself; the method predates and will outlive its current tooling.

Recommended deployment: Generative Research Methods as the formal credential name, AI-Augmented Research Methods as the marketing subtitle while that wave lasts, Framework Engineering as the practitioner title, and the other two registers doing their jobs where they live.

## The Five Axioms

Every course, assessment, and practice in this curriculum descends from five axioms. As with any honest axiom set, each was extracted from a specific failure or success in practice, and the origin is stated so students know these are scars, not decorations.

**Axiom 1: Everything you can think exists. Nothing you think is valid yet.**
Generation is licensed without limit; validity is a separate property assigned only by external grounding. This axiom does double work: it removes the fear that throttles generation, and it removes the self-congratulation that skips testing. Origin: a set-theoretic framework whose core move was separating comprehension from stability, later found to govern its author's entire corpus practice.

**Axiom 2: Hold no grudge against paradox.**
Contradictory variants are inventory, not errors. Rival versions of a framework are stored side by side, unresolved, until an external test does the choosing, because the rival you resolved away is the answer you no longer have. Origin: a cosmological framework whose two contradictory drafts, kept against all tidy instinct, turned out to be the two terms of the equation the data selected a year later.

**Axiom 3: Write the myth before the math.**
Every major idea gets encoded at three registers: myth (highest compression, lowest precision), framework (the working middle), formalism (lowest compression, highest precision). The registers are not presentation styles. They are redundant storage: when a framework register dies under test, the myth register survives and decompresses differently. Origin: a mythology written months before the method's biggest testing day, which turned out to already contain the method's theology, including the ledger, deified.

**Axiom 4: Name the death before the test.**
No claim touches data, market, or audience without a pre-registered kill condition: an observable, dated, third-party-checkable outcome that ends the claim. A claim without a named death is decoration. Revisions after a death must clear three bars at once: keep everything the prior version fit, explain the wound, and predict something the revision was not built to satisfy. Origin: a physics session in which two model versions were executed by conditions written before the data loaded, and the executions were what made the survivor credible.

**Axiom 5: The ledger is the method.**
Failures are kept, numbered, dated, and published beside successes, because witnessed failure is what makes results irreversible and testimony trustworthy. Synthesis draws from the ledger's survivors; credibility draws from its dead. A researcher's graveyard, honestly kept, is worth more than most researchers' trophies. Origin: every artifact the method has produced that strangers took seriously led with its failures, and every artifact that hid them would not have deserved the attention.

## The Course of Study

Five terms. Each term embodies one axiom, and the sequence is the method's loop run once, slowly, on the student's own ideas. Total design length: two years part-time or one year full-time, though the structure ports to a certificate (Terms 1 through 3 compressed) or a full degree concentration (each term expanded to a semester with domain electives).

### Term 0: The Stance (gate term)

Before generation, calibration. The student learns to hold beliefs as probabilistic, to notice motivated reasoning in themselves, and to treat being wrong as information rather than injury.

Core readings: Galef, *The Scout Mindset*. Tetlock, *Superforecasting*. Sagan, *The Demon-Haunted World*. Polya, *How to Solve It*.
Practice: a personal forecasting log, thirty predictions with confidence levels, scored at term's end. The student meets their own calibration curve before they meet anything else.
Gate: no student proceeds who cannot state, in writing, one belief they changed during the term and what evidence changed it.

### Term 1: Generation (Axiom 1)

Abduction and analogical structure-mapping, taught as trainable skills rather than gifts. The student learns to lift relational skeletons out of one domain and install them in another, and to generate without the internal critic that kills variants at birth.

Core readings: Hofstadter and Sander, *Surfaces and Essences*. Lakoff and Johnson, *Metaphors We Live By*. Gentner, "Structure-Mapping: A Theoretical Framework for Analogy" (1983). Fauconnier and Turner, *The Way We Think*. Epstein, *Range*. Schön, "Generative Metaphor" (1979). Gärdenfors, *Conceptual Spaces: The Geometry of Thought* (2000), read as the academic formalization of the same territory the practice manual maps in first person.
Practice manual: Pendry, *The Node-Space Navigation Guide*, the term's operating instructions. Its five-week protocol (passive drift, directional pull, resistance mapping, capacity management, ambient tagging) runs in parallel with the framework production below, and its Three-Breath Method is the standard synthesis-under-pressure drill. The guide's transferability carries its own pre-registered condition, stated in Term 3's materials, because a manual of one mind's mechanics must earn the claim that other minds share the architecture.
Practice: ten frameworks in ten weeks, one per week, in ten different domains, with a hard rule that no framework may be tested, criticized, or revised during the term. Generation and selection are separated on purpose, because students who test while generating produce three timid frameworks instead of ten live ones.
Assessment: volume, domain spread, and structural depth of the mappings. Correctness is not assessed. Correctness does not exist yet.

### Term 2: The Population (Axioms 2 and 3)

Corpus-keeping as a discipline. The student learns to store rivals without resolving them, to version drafts instead of overwriting them, and to encode each major idea at all three registers.

Core readings: Feyerabend, *Against Method*. Kuhn, *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions*. Campbell, "Blind Variation and Selective Retention" (1960). Kripke, "Outline of a Theory of Truth" (1975). Case study: Kepler's *Mysterium Cosmographicum* read against the later laws, myth first, selection second, formalism last, four centuries before anyone named the pipeline.
Practice: the student takes their Term 1 frameworks and deliberately writes a contradicting rival for at least three of them, storing both without adjudication. Each of the ten gets its three registers written: the myth (one page, any form: verse, parable, invocation), the framework (claims, exclusions, and the boundary between them), and one page of formalism, however rough.
Assessment: register fidelity, meaning the same structural content survives translation across all three registers, graded by a reader who sees the registers separately and must match them.

### Term 3: Selection (Axiom 4)

The term with teeth. Statistics, pre-registration, and contact with external reality. The student's own Term 1 population goes to the wall.

Core readings: McElreath, *Statistical Rethinking*. Mayo, *Statistical Inference as Severe Testing*. Lakatos, "Falsification and the Methodology of Scientific Research Programmes" (1970). Fitzpatrick, *The Mom Test* (for market-facing claims). Hammack, *Book of Proof*, chapters 1 through 7 (for formal claims).
Practice: every testable framework from the student's population gets a filed Kill Condition Protocol (specified below) and then meets its test: public dataset, market conversations, formal proof attempt, or replication, matched to the claim's type.
Hard requirement: at least one framework must die during the term, documented in full. A student whose entire population survives has either tested dishonestly or generated timidly, and the term is repeated either way.
Assessment: quality of the pre-registrations, severity of the tests, and honesty of the ledger. Survival of frameworks carries zero grade weight.

### Term 4: Synthesis and Transmission (Axiom 5)

Composition from survivors, and the method's hardest test: surviving transmission. The student assembles working structure from the components their selection round validated, writes the full three-register account, and teaches the loop to one person from outside the program.

Core readings: Deutsch, *The Beginning of Infinity*. Hamming, *The Art of Doing Science and Engineering*. Bainbridge, "Ironies of Automation" (1983), read as the warning about what tool-augmented method does to the unaugmented practitioner.
Capstone, in place of a thesis: **The Ledger**. A complete documented corpus covering the student's two years: every framework born, every register written, every pre-registration filed, every death with its F-number, every synthesis with its component lineage, and a final section in the student's own three registers describing what the method did to how they think.
Transmission requirement: the student's apprentice runs one full loop, generation through ledger, without the student's intervention. The boost clears when the apprentice's ledger stands on its own.

## Sample Syllabus: Term 3 Core Course

**GRM 301: Selection and the Kill Condition** (14 weeks)

Weeks 1 and 2: Calibration revisited. Severity as the measure of a test: what would this test catch if the claim were false? Reading: Mayo, chapters 1 and 2. Assignment: rate the severity of five published studies' designs.
Weeks 3 through 5: Pre-registration as craft. Writing observable, dated, third-party-checkable kill conditions. The three-bar rule for post-death revision. Reading: Lakatos essay. Assignment: file Kill Condition Protocols for every testable framework in your population; peer review of each other's conditions for escape hatches.
Weeks 6 through 9: Contact. Each student runs their tests: statistical fits against public data (McElreath chapters run in parallel), market conversations under Mom Test rules, or proof attempts under Hammack. Weekly ledger entries due, deaths filed within 48 hours of occurrence.
Weeks 10 and 11: The autopsy seminar. Each student presents one death to the cohort: what the condition was, how it triggered, what the wound localizes to, what survives. The cohort's job is to find hidden rescues and unearned survivals. Paired instrument: resistance mapping from the Node-Space guide, run on the dead framework itself. The Kill Condition Protocol harvests a failure at the external layer; resistance mapping harvests the same failure at the pre-linguistic layer, asking where the idea repelled its own components before the data ever arrived, and whether the internal repulsion predicted the external death. Students who find that it did have located their gradient sense; students who find that it did not have located a blind spot, and both findings enter the ledger.
Weeks 12 and 13: Revision under the three bars. Students attempt one resurrection each; most should fail, and a documented failed resurrection earns full credit.
Week 14: Ledger submission and the term's single exam question, answered in one page: "Which of your beliefs about your own work did this term execute, and what is the evidence?"

Grading: pre-registration quality 30 percent, test severity 30 percent, ledger honesty 30 percent, autopsy presentation 10 percent. Framework survival: 0 percent.

## The Register Discipline: Development Specification

The practice, specified so it can be taught rather than absorbed.

**The myth template.** One page maximum. Any form that compresses: parable, invocation, figure, verse. Must contain the idea's central mechanism as image or story, and must be writable by someone who cannot yet state the mechanism precisely. The test of a good myth register: a stranger who reads only the myth can predict what the framework will claim.

**The framework template.** For each claim: what it asserts, what it excludes, and the boundary between them stated as something observable. Rivals welcome and stored unresolved. This is the working register, the one that meets tests.

**The formalism template.** Definitions, one derivation or proof sketch, and at least one falsifiable statement with units or checkable structure. One page suffices at first. The rule that matters: the formalism is written last, and its absence never blocks the other two registers from shipping.

**Cadence:** all three registers, dated, for every major idea, with the explicit understanding that most formalisms will be thin and that thinness is acceptable. The redundancy is the point, not the polish.

**Grading rubric, register fidelity:** a reader receives the three registers of one idea shuffled among the registers of other ideas and must reassemble the triples. Fidelity is the reassembly rate. High-fidelity registers carry the same skeleton at three compressions; low-fidelity registers are three unrelated documents wearing one title.

## The Kill Condition Protocol

The form filed before any claim meets reality. Every field mandatory.

1. **Claim**, one sentence, framework register.
2. **Register of origin**, and links to the other two registers if written.
3. **The test**: dataset, market, proof, or replication, named specifically. "Further research" is not a test.
4. **Kill condition**: the observable outcome that ends the claim. Must be dated, quantified where possible, and checkable by a third party who dislikes you.
5. **Prize condition**: the outcome that would convert a parameter into a prediction or a hunch into a result. Named in advance so success cannot be redefined afterward.
6. **Forbidden rescues**: the post-hoc moves the author pre-commits not to make. Standard entries: no reinterpreting the kill condition, no invoking untested auxiliary mechanisms as saves, no "the test was unfair" after agreeing it was fair.
7. **The three-bar clause** for any revision after death: keep all prior fits, explain the wound mechanistically, and predict at least one thing the revision was not constructed to produce.

**Verdict vocabulary**, applied at test's end: KILL (condition triggered, claim ends), WOUND (condition partially triggered, claim survives with a named injury and a mandatory revision clock), TIE (indistinguishable from the null or the incumbent; claim survives but earns nothing), CROWN (prize condition met).

**Ledger entry format:** F-numbers for failures, sequential, permanent, never deleted. Each entry: date, claim, condition, trigger, localization (which assumption the death traces to), and what survives. The ledger is public by default, because a private graveyard protects the author and a public one protects the reader.

**Worked example, canonical:** a cosmological feedback mechanism committed in advance to a positive sign on its growth parameter; the pre-registered condition stated that ruler data confirming the opposite drift would end it; the data arrived, the cost of the committed sign exceeded three sigma, the claim was killed as F5 within the hour, the wound localized to one clause, and the rival clause from the same corpus was crowned by the identical dataset. Total elapsed time from commitment to execution: one afternoon. That afternoon is what this entire curriculum exists to make repeatable.

## Assessment Philosophy, Stated Once

You cannot fail this program by being wrong. You can only fail it by hiding. Graduation requires a ledger containing no fewer than three documented deaths, at least one failed resurrection, and one transmission. A student who arrives brilliant and leaves brilliant has wasted the tuition; the program exists to install the loop, and the loop's signature is a graveyard the graduate shows strangers without flinching.
